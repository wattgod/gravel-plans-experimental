How Training Works

The Adaptation Cycle

All training follows the same basic cycle: Stress ‚Üí Fatigue ‚Üí Recovery ‚Üí Adaptation.

Step 1: Stress

You apply training stress‚Äîa hard workout that exceeds your current capacity. This creates disruption your body needs to solve.

Step 2: Fatigue

Immediately after training, you're weaker than before. Fatigue is the signal that triggers adaptation.

Step 3: Recovery

Given adequate rest, nutrition, and time, your body repairs and rebuilds stronger than before.

Step 4: Adaptation

Supercompensation: your body builds more capacity than it had before to handle similar stress more easily next time.

Where It Goes Wrong

Insufficient stress: Workout too easy, no adaptation triggered.
Insufficient recovery: New stress before recovery complete, accumulated fatigue without adaptation.
Inconsistent stress: Heroic efforts followed by weeks off‚Äînever compound adaptations.

Workout Execution

Universal Execution Rules

  1. Warm up properly ‚Äî 15-20 minutes progressive before intensity
  2. Do the actual workout ‚Äî Not more, not less. Trust the plan.
  3. Chase time-in-zone ‚Äî Highest average across the set, not hero intervals
  4. Stop if power drops >10% ‚Äî Quality beats quantity

G Spot Execution (88-92% FTP)

This is gravel race pace‚Äîuncomfortably sustainable.

  • Stay seated, control breathing
  • Hard enough to hurt, easy enough to repeat
  • Could talk in short phrases but wouldn't want to
  • Fuel properly: 60-80g carbs/hour
Common G Spot Mistake

Starting at 95% FTP because "it doesn't feel that hard yet." By minute 30, you're dying. Start at 88% and build only if holding that feels too easy.

Recovery

Recovery Makes You Fast

Training creates stress. Recovery creates adaptation. Skip recovery, skip adaptation.

Sleep

7-9 hours minimum. This is when growth hormone peaks and muscles rebuild. Non-negotiable.

Nutrition

Protein within 30 min post-workout. Carbs to replenish glycogen. Real food over supplements.

Active Recovery

Easy spinning, walking, stretching. Movement promotes blood flow without adding stress.

Recovery Weeks

Every 3-4 weeks, volume drops 30-40%. This is when adaptation consolidates. Don't skip.

Technical Skills for Unbound Gravel 200

Fitness gets you to the race. Skills determine whether you use that fitness efficiently.

Skill 1: Loose Surface Cornering

Look through the turn, weight your outside pedal, point inside knee toward the turn.

Cue: "Eyes through, weight down, knee out"

Skill 2: Eating While Riding

Practice on every long ride. Start flat, graduate to rough terrain.

Cue: "Flat road first, both hands work"

Skill 3: Group Riding

Smooth, predictable movements. No sudden braking or surging. Communicate.

Cue: "Smooth pulls, steady pace, speak up"

Skill 4: Descending on Loose Gravel

Weight back, elbows bent and loose, grip relaxed. Brake before turns, not during.

Cue: "Weight back, light hands, eyes up"

Skill 5: Pacing Long Efforts

Why: 200 miles requires perfect pacing. Going too hard early means suffering later. Going too easy means losing time.

How: Practice holding G Spot power for 2+ hours during long rides. Learn to read your body's signals. Use power and HR together, not power alone.

Cue: "Steady power, check in every hour"

Race-Specific Skill Notes

Cornering on loose gravel is critical. Practice eating while riding. Group riding skills save massive energy over 200 miles.

Your gut is trainable. Start at 40-50g/hr, increase by 10g every 2-3 weeks. By race day, you should handle 80-100g/hr comfortably.

Mental Training

Three Tools That Work

6-2-7 Breathing

Inhale 6 seconds, hold 2, exhale 7. Triggers parasympathetic response, calms panic. Practice until automatic.

Performance Statements

Pre-planned phrases that replace negative self-talk. "This is supposed to be hard." "Just get to the next aid station."

Personal Highlight Reel

Mental movie of past success + race execution + crossing the finish. Triggers confident emotional state on demand.

Crisis Protocol

When things go wrong (they will): Breathe (3-5 cycles 6-2-7) ‚Üí Statement (your most powerful one) ‚Üí Action (smallest possible next step)

Race Day

The Three-Act Structure

PhaseWhenYour Job
THE MADNESS0-90 minSurvive. Find sustainable group. Eat and drink while others forget.
FALSE DAWN90 min - 3 hrsStay fueled. Contribute without overworking. Bank energy.
THE PIPER3+ hrs to finishExecute your plan while others fall apart. This is the race.

Unbound Gravel 200-Specific Tactics

Survive the first 60 miles. Find a sustainable group. Fuel every 20 minutes. Don't chase attacks. The race happens after mile 100 when others fade.

Aid Station Strategy

Aid stations every 30-40 miles. Don't skip them. Refill bottles, grab real food, take 2-3 minutes to reset. The time lost is worth the energy gained.

Tires

The Decision Framework

Stop overthinking. Use this:

  1. What width fits? When in doubt, go 38-42mm
  2. What's durable? Ask people who've raced it
  3. Can you test it? Mounted 2+ weeks before race day
  4. Does it feel good? 3-4 hour test ride
Common Mistakes

‚Ä¢ Buying new tires race week
‚Ä¢ Chasing marginal gains over reliability
‚Ä¢ Ignoring your own testing data

The actual best tire is the one that gets you to the finish line without flatting and doesn't beat you up for 8 hours.

Glossary

FTP (Functional Threshold Power)
Highest power you can sustain for ~1 hour. All zones calculated as % of FTP.
TSS (Training Stress Score)
Training load based on duration √ó intensity. 100 TSS = 1 hour at FTP.
G Spot (88-92% FTP)
Gravel race pace. Uncomfortably sustainable. Where you'll spend most of the race.
RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion)
1-10 scale of how hard effort feels. Trust RPE when devices conflict.
Polarized Training
80% easy (Z1-Z2), 20% hard (Z4+), minimal middle zone.
Taper
Volume drops 30-50% before race while intensity maintains. Arrive fresh.
Bonk
Severe glycogen depletion. Prevention: eat early, eat often.
Supercompensation
Body builds more capacity than before to handle similar stress more easily.
